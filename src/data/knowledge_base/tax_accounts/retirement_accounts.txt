Retirement Accounts: 401(k), IRA, Roth, and More

Tax-advantaged retirement accounts are among the most powerful wealth-building tools available to U.S. investors. Understanding the different types and their rules is essential for maximizing after-tax wealth.

401(k) PLANS

What It Is:
An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan. Contributions come from pre-tax dollars, reducing current taxable income. Investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal.

2024 Contribution Limits:
- Employee contribution: $23,000/year (under age 50)
- Catch-up contribution (age 50+): Additional $7,500/year (total $30,500)
- Total including employer contributions: $69,000/year

Key Features:
- Employer Match: Many employers match 50-100% of employee contributions up to a percentage of salary. This is free money — always contribute at least enough to get the full match.
- Vesting: Employer contributions may vest over time (e.g., 20% per year over 5 years)
- Investment Options: Limited to fund menu offered by plan provider
- Early Withdrawal: 10% penalty plus ordinary income tax before age 59½
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): Must begin by age 73

Roth 401(k):
After-tax contributions; tax-free growth and withdrawals. Available at many employers. Same contribution limits as traditional 401(k). No RMDs during owner's lifetime (starting 2024).

INDIVIDUAL RETIREMENT ACCOUNTS (IRAs)

Traditional IRA:
Pre-tax contributions (if income-eligible); tax-deferred growth; taxed at withdrawal.
2024 limit: $7,000/year ($8,000 if age 50+)
Deductibility phases out for high incomes if covered by a workplace plan.

Roth IRA:
After-tax contributions; tax-free growth; tax-free qualified withdrawals.
2024 income limits: Phase-out begins at $146,000 (single) / $230,000 (married filing jointly)
No RMDs. Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn at any time without penalty.

Backdoor Roth IRA:
High-income earners who exceed Roth IRA income limits can make non-deductible Traditional IRA contributions and then convert to Roth. Consult a tax professional.

ROTH vs. TRADITIONAL: The Decision Framework

Choose Roth When:
- You're in a low tax bracket now (expect to be in higher bracket at retirement)
- You're young (more time for tax-free compounding)
- You want flexibility (no RMDs, penalty-free contribution withdrawal)

Choose Traditional When:
- You're in a high tax bracket now (want the deduction today)
- You expect lower income in retirement
- Your state has high income taxes now but you plan to move to a no-tax state at retirement

HEALTH SAVINGS ACCOUNT (HSA)

Available to those with High-Deductible Health Plans (HDHPs). Triple tax advantage:
1. Contributions are pre-tax
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

2024 limits: $4,150 (individual) / $8,300 (family)
After age 65, can withdraw for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, like a Traditional IRA).
HSA is the most tax-efficient account available — maximize it if you can afford to pay medical expenses out of pocket.

SOLO 401(k) AND SEP-IRA (FOR SELF-EMPLOYED)

Solo 401(k): For self-employed with no employees. Can contribute as both employer and employee.
2024 limit: Up to $69,000 / $76,500 (age 50+)

SEP-IRA: Simpler administration; contributions up to 25% of compensation, max $69,000.

OPTIMAL CONTRIBUTION ORDER (TAX EFFICIENCY)

1. 401(k) contributions up to employer match (100% instant return)
2. HSA (if eligible) — triple tax advantage
3. Max out Roth IRA (tax-free compounding)
4. Max out 401(k) beyond the match
5. Taxable brokerage account

This order maximizes tax efficiency while ensuring you capture all employer-provided benefits.
