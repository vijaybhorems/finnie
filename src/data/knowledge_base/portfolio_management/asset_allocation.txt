Asset Allocation: Matching Investments to Goals

Asset allocation — the division of a portfolio among different asset classes — is widely considered the most important investment decision. Studies by Brinson, Hood, and Beebower found that asset allocation explains over 90% of the variability in portfolio returns over time.

Core Asset Classes:

Equities (Stocks): 7-10% historical annual returns; high volatility; best for long time horizons.
Fixed Income (Bonds): 2-5% historical annual returns; lower volatility; capital preservation.
Cash & Equivalents: Money market funds, T-bills; minimal returns but maximum liquidity and safety.
Real Assets: Real estate, commodities, infrastructure; inflation protection.
Alternative Investments: Hedge funds, private equity, venture capital; typically for accredited investors.

Age-Based Allocation Rules of Thumb:

The "Rule of 100" (now updated to Rule of 110 or 120 given longer lifespans):
Stock allocation ≈ 110 - Your Age
A 30-year-old: ~80% stocks, 20% bonds
A 60-year-old: ~50% stocks, 50% bonds

Target Date Funds apply this concept automatically — a 2055 Target Date Fund starts equity-heavy and automatically shifts toward bonds as 2055 approaches.

Strategic vs. Tactical Asset Allocation:
Strategic allocation sets long-term targets based on risk tolerance and goals, with periodic rebalancing. Tactical allocation makes short-term adjustments based on market conditions — evidence for systematic outperformance is weak for most investors.

Rebalancing:
Over time, outperforming assets grow to exceed their target weights, increasing risk beyond intended levels. A portfolio targeting 60/40 stocks/bonds might drift to 70/30 after a bull market. Rebalancing restores target weights by selling outperformers and buying underperformers.

Rebalancing strategies:
- Calendar-based: Quarterly or annual review
- Threshold-based: Rebalance when any asset class drifts more than 5% from target
- Tax-aware: Use new contributions to rebalance in taxable accounts to avoid capital gains

Risk Tolerance vs. Risk Capacity:
Risk tolerance: Psychological comfort with portfolio fluctuations
Risk capacity: Financial ability to absorb losses

Someone may have high risk tolerance (not lose sleep over volatility) but low risk capacity (needs the money in 2 years). The lower of the two should determine allocation.

Sample Allocations by Risk Profile:

Conservative (5-10 year horizon, low risk):
- 30% U.S. stocks, 10% international stocks, 50% bonds, 10% cash

Moderate (10-20 year horizon, medium risk):
- 45% U.S. stocks, 15% international stocks, 35% bonds, 5% cash

Aggressive (20+ year horizon, high risk):
- 60% U.S. stocks, 25% international stocks, 10% bonds, 5% REITs

The Role of Glide Path:
As investors approach their goal date (retirement, home purchase), they should gradually reduce equity exposure — the "glide path." This reduces the sequence-of-returns risk: a major market crash just before retirement can devastate portfolios if they're still heavily in stocks.
