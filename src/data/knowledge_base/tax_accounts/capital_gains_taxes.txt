Capital Gains Taxes: What Every Investor Must Know

Capital gains taxes apply when you sell an investment for more than you paid. Understanding the rules can significantly impact your after-tax investment returns.

SHORT-TERM vs. LONG-TERM CAPITAL GAINS

Short-Term Capital Gains:
Assets held for ONE YEAR OR LESS. Taxed as ordinary income (same rate as your salary).
Tax rates: 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37% depending on taxable income.

Long-Term Capital Gains:
Assets held for MORE THAN ONE YEAR. Preferential tax rates:
- 0%: Single filers with taxable income up to $47,025; Married filing jointly up to $94,050 (2024)
- 15%: Most middle and upper-middle income earners
- 20%: Single filers above $518,900; MFJ above $583,750 (2024)

The Holding Period Advantage:
Selling after one year can reduce your tax rate dramatically. A high-income investor selling after 11 months pays 37% federal tax; waiting one more month reduces it to 20% — a 17 percentage point difference.

NET INVESTMENT INCOME TAX (NIIT)

An additional 3.8% tax on investment income (including capital gains) for high earners:
- Single filers with Modified AGI above $200,000
- Married filing jointly above $250,000

This means high earners may pay up to 23.8% on long-term capital gains (20% + 3.8%).

COST BASIS

Cost basis is what you paid for an investment, including commissions. Capital gain = Sale price - Cost basis.

Cost Basis Methods:
- FIFO (First In, First Out): Default for most brokers. Oldest shares sold first.
- Specific Identification: Choose exactly which shares to sell. Allows selling highest-cost shares to minimize gains.
- Average Cost: Used for mutual funds. Average of all purchase prices.

Choosing the right method can minimize taxes significantly. If you have shares purchased at various prices, specific identification allows you to sell the most expensive shares first, minimizing your gain.

TAX-LOSS HARVESTING

Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains — a powerful tax-planning strategy.

How It Works:
1. You have $10,000 in capital gains from selling Stock A
2. Stock B is down $4,000 from your purchase price
3. Selling Stock B creates a $4,000 capital loss
4. Your net taxable gain is reduced to $6,000

Rules:
- Capital losses first offset capital gains (short-term losses offset short-term gains first, then long-term)
- Excess losses up to $3,000/year can offset ordinary income
- Remaining losses carry forward indefinitely to future years

Wash-Sale Rule:
You cannot buy the same (or substantially identical) security within 30 days before or after selling it at a loss. The loss is disallowed. Solution: Buy a similar but not identical ETF (e.g., sell VTI, buy SCHB to maintain market exposure).

QUALIFIED DIVIDENDS

Dividends from U.S. companies and qualified foreign companies held for the required holding period are taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20%) rather than ordinary income rates. "Non-qualified" dividends are taxed as ordinary income.

INHERITED ASSETS AND STEPPED-UP BASIS

Assets inherited at death receive a "step-up" in cost basis to the fair market value at the date of death. This eliminates all unrealized capital gains — an enormous benefit for heirs.

Example: Your parent bought Apple at $10/share (now worth $200). You inherit and immediately sell at $200. Your gain is $0 — basis stepped up to $200 at death.

TAX-EFFICIENT INVESTING STRATEGIES

1. Hold index funds and ETFs in taxable accounts (low turnover = fewer taxable events)
2. Hold high-yield bonds and REITs in tax-advantaged accounts (ordinary income)
3. Maximize tax-advantaged accounts before taxable accounts
4. Use tax-loss harvesting systematically in taxable accounts
5. Consider asset location: high-growth assets in Roth, income-producing in Traditional
