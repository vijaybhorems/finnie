Understanding Investment Risk

Risk is the possibility that an investment's actual return will differ from its expected return — including the possibility of losing all or part of the invested capital. Effective investing requires understanding, measuring, and managing different types of risk.

TYPES OF INVESTMENT RISK

Market Risk (Systematic Risk):
The risk that the entire market declines, affecting all investments regardless of quality. Cannot be diversified away. Sources: recessions, interest rate changes, geopolitical events, pandemics.
Measurement: Beta (sensitivity to market movements). VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures implied market volatility.

Specific Risk (Unsystematic Risk):
Risk specific to a particular company or industry. A company's stock falls because of an earnings miss, CEO scandal, or product recall. Can be reduced through diversification. Owning 20+ uncorrelated stocks eliminates most specific risk.

Interest Rate Risk:
The risk that rising interest rates reduce the value of fixed-income investments. Most relevant for bonds: a 1% rate increase causes roughly a 7-8% loss on a 10-year bond.

Inflation Risk (Purchasing Power Risk):
The risk that investment returns don't keep pace with inflation. A savings account earning 0.5% when inflation is 3% generates a -2.5% real return. Equities and TIPS mitigate inflation risk better than cash or nominal bonds.

Liquidity Risk:
The risk of not being able to sell an investment quickly at a fair price. Real estate, private equity, and small-cap stocks have higher liquidity risk than large-cap stocks or U.S. Treasuries.

Credit/Default Risk:
For bonds: the risk that the issuer cannot make interest payments or repay principal. Rated by agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch). Investment-grade bonds (BBB- and above) have historically low default rates.

Currency Risk:
For international investments: the risk that currency exchange rate changes reduce returns. A U.S. investor earning 8% in European stocks loses value if the euro weakens against the dollar.

Concentration Risk:
Holding too much in a single stock, sector, or geography. Enron employees who held large company stock positions lost both their jobs and retirement savings when the company collapsed.

Sequence of Returns Risk:
The risk that the order of investment returns matters, especially near retirement. Retiring into a bear market forces selling assets at depressed prices to fund living expenses, permanently impairing the portfolio. This is why asset allocation should become more conservative as retirement approaches.

MEASURING RISK

Standard Deviation:
The most common measure of portfolio volatility. The S&P 500 has historically shown ~15% annual standard deviation. Higher standard deviation = wider range of outcomes.

Sharpe Ratio:
(Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Portfolio Standard Deviation
Measures return per unit of risk. Higher is better. The S&P 500's long-run Sharpe ratio is approximately 0.4-0.5.

Maximum Drawdown:
The largest peak-to-trough decline. The S&P 500's worst drawdown was -57% during 2007-2009. Investors must be able to stomach their portfolio's maximum drawdown psychologically.

Value at Risk (VaR):
The maximum expected loss over a period at a given confidence level. "95% VaR of 5%" means there's a 5% chance of losing more than 5% in a single day.

RISK TOLERANCE ASSESSMENT

Factors determining appropriate risk tolerance:
- Time horizon: Longer time horizons allow more risk (time to recover from drawdowns)
- Income stability: Stable employment allows more portfolio risk
- Savings rate: High savers can take more portfolio risk (contributions buffer losses)
- Liquidity needs: Near-term needs require less volatile assets
- Psychological tolerance: Can you sleep at night with a 30% drawdown?

A simple test: If your $100,000 portfolio dropped to $70,000 (-30%) in 6 months, would you:
a) Buy more (you can take high risk)
b) Stay the course (moderate risk tolerance)
c) Sell (you need a more conservative allocation)
